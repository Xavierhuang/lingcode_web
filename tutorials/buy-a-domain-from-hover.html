Buy a domain from Hover

Hover positions itself as the registrar for people who want a domain and only a domain. The checkout has no upsells, the support is competent, the UI is calm. You pay slightly more than budget registrars; you get less friction in return.

Within the "no bullshit" registrar category, there's a smaller subset that doesn't even try to be the cheapest. Hover is the cleanest example. Their pitch is that they sell domains and email — nothing else — and they don't bury upsells. The result is a checkout experience that's noticeably calmer than GoDaddy and a bit more polished than Porkbun, at prices a few dollars higher than the budget tier.

The user this fits is someone who values the time they don't spend dodging cross-sells more than they value the $3/year difference in registration price. For one or two personal domains, that math works. For a portfolio of fifty, it doesn't — at scale the price difference becomes real money and Cloudflare or Porkbun makes more sense.

Hover is owned by Tucows, the same parent company that runs OpenSRS and one of the largest backend registrar operations in the world. So while the brand is small, the underlying infrastructure is industrial-grade. That's part of what you're paying for: stable corporate ownership and good support, not a one-person operation.

Step 1: Search and price-check

1

Same-price-every-year, like the better registrars

Search at hover.com. The price displayed is the renewal price; no first-year discount, no asterisks. For .com expect roughly $15–17 — about $5 more than Cloudflare, about $2 more than Namecheap's renewal year. Other common TLDs follow the same pattern: slightly premium, transparent.

Hover supports a broad range of TLDs, including many country-codes (.co.uk, .ca, .de, etc.). For specialty TLDs they may not be the cheapest option, but they handle the regional registration requirements cleanly.

Step 2: Add to cart, checkout, done

2

One line item

The cart shows your domain. WHOIS privacy is included free. Email forwarding (one address, for inbound only) is included free. There's no SSL upsell, no hosting upsell, no website builder. Email mailboxes are sold as a separate product if you want them; they're not pushed at checkout.

Enter contact info, payment, confirm. The whole transaction is shorter than reading this paragraph.

Hover Email vs. forwarding: the free email forwarding sends inbound mail to your existing inbox. Hover also sells real mailboxes (send-and-receive, with webmail and IMAP) as an add-on for ~$20/year per mailbox. If you want a sendable hello@yourdomain.com, this is one option — though Fastmail or Google Workspace are often better values.

Step 3: The DNS panel

3

Unsurprising, in a good way

Hover's DNS interface is a standard list of records with inline editing — A, AAAA, CNAME, MX, TXT, SRV. Nothing creative; nothing in the way. Add records, save, done.

For setup specifics — what record types map to what destinations, propagation behavior, verification — see Connect your domain to a server. The DNS work itself is the same at every registrar; the only difference is which UI you're clicking through.

Step 4: Support — the actual differentiator

4

Real humans, North American hours

Hover's actual selling point — the thing nobody else in the budget tier really does — is competent telephone and email support. If a domain transfer gets stuck, if a TLD-specific registration requirement isn't obvious, if you need to recover an account, you can call a person who knows the system. The wait time is short and the support agent has authority to fix things.

Most developers don't need this. The minority who do — usually when a domain is critical to a business and something's gone wrong — find it worth the price difference.

Step 5: When to pick someone else

5

Price-sensitive use cases

  • Many domains. Five domains at Hover vs. five at Cloudflare/Porkbun is $25–50/year more. Across decades, that's noticeable. At scale, optimize for price.
  • Throwaway or experimental domains. If you might not keep the name past year one, paying premium prices for support you won't need doesn't fit. Use the cheapest registrar.
  • API-heavy automation. Hover has no public API. If you want to manage domains programmatically, this is a hard no — Porkbun, Cloudflare, Gandi, or AWS all have APIs; Hover doesn't.

Step 6: After purchase

6

Standard next steps

  • Point DNS at where your site lives — see DNS setup.
  • Enable 2FA in account settings.
  • If using Magic Deploy, follow the host's DNS instructions — usually one or two records.
No programmatic access: Hover doesn't offer a public API. If a tool you're using assumes registrar-side automation (Terraform-managed DNS, an agent script that updates records), Hover won't fit. Plan accordingly before transferring an automation-heavy domain in.
